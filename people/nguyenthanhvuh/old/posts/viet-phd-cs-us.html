Having been involved in Ph.D. admissions for many years and having numerous discussions with international (especially Vietnamese) students interested in Ph.D. programs in Computer Science in the US, I've realized that many international students lack a clear understanding of the admission process at US universities. This confusion not only discourages students from applying but also creates the perception that getting accepted is difficult compared to Ph.D. programs in other universities.

So I want to share my personal opinions and recommendations for international students who are interested in applying for a Ph.D. in Computer Science in the US. While I wrote this with Vietnamese applicants in mind, my observation and advice should be applicable to general international students.

If you have suggestions or comments, feel free to create a Github issue for discussion.

1. Funding and Should you apply?

First and foremost, I want to emphasize that Ph.D. students in Computer Science do not need to worry about funding, especially at R1 universities in the US. If you are admitted, you will almost certainly receive full funding to support your studies, including tuition, health insurance, and a monthly stipend. Depending on the university, you may even receive additional benefits like summer salary. Note that this funding is typically available for Ph.D. students and not for Master's students.

at GMU, in addition to the usual tuition waiving and health insurance, admitted students are guaranteed with a GTA stipend (graduate teaching assistantship) which is $28K/9-month for 4 years and a 3-month summer salary ($9K, so $37K first year).

Second, I believe that applying to a good US university is not any harder than at good schools in other countries. If you think you have a chance at schools in other countries, then you surely have a chance in the US as well.

One of the reasons I create this post is that several CS faculty has asked me to help recruit Vietnamese students. In general the number of CS Ph.D applications to universities in the US is very low (compared to other countries such as India and China) and I personally would like to see more applications from Vietnam.

2. Application

How is your application evaluated? After you submit your Ph.D. application (usually in mid-December), it will be first screened for general requirements (e.g., did you submit things that are required, did you pay application fee etc). And then it will be reviewed by a Ph.D. admission committee consisting of faculty members in the department. Each application is typically reviewed by about three faculty members who assess various aspects, such as transcripts and letters of recommendation, to reach a consensus about your case.

At GMU, we usually decide that a candidate is either (i) accepted with funding (GTA or GRA) or (ii) rejected. In other words, either we either accept you with full funding, or we don't. In some rare cases, we accept you with no funding because you have funding on your own (e.g., you are supported by your government or have external grants). Also, we justify our decision with a short summary about your application and in it list strengths (e.g., well-known undergrad school) and weaknesses (e.g., weak LoRs).

It is worth noting that most Ph.D. committee members are new assistant professors. This is designed to give junior faculty, who don't have students yet, the opportunities to recruit students. The chair of the committee will be a tenured/senior faculty, but they typically will not review your papers and instead assign it to other committee members. So, keep that in mind.

The primary focus of the admissions committee is to evaluate your background and interest in research since a Ph.D. in Computer Science is a research degree. To assess your research capability, they consider several key indicators:

2.1. Research ability.

The most effective evidence of research ability is having published papers in reputable international conferences and journals. It's important to aim for the top conferences in your field, which you can find on places such as CSRankings, designed specifically to help PhD applicants. Local conferences and non-English journals or conferences do not carry as much weight since their quality is often unknown to US faculty. However, I understand that most Vietnamese students (even professors) do not have the opportunities to publish in top places, so general IEEE/ACM/USENIX confs/journals would suffice.

Additionally, work or internship experiences at renowned research laboratories, such as Microsoft Research, can significantly enhance your application. However, working at local companies or research places that are not widely known might not have the same impact. Top research places in Vietnam, e.g., VinAI, unfortunately are still relatively unknown to most universities in the US. It's getting more well-known though, but do not assume US faculty know about them, i.e., you should explicit say something about them in your statement.

Lastly, if you have participated in top international programming competitions, it can also demonstrate your research potential, especially if you want to do research in topics that "build" stuff, e.g., my area in software engineering, formal methods.

2.2. Letters of recommendation (LoRs).

Having a letter from an internationally recognized researcher can greatly strengthen your application. However, obtaining such a letter can be challenging. It's acceptable to have a letter from any professor who knows you well enough to talk about your specific research experience and capabilities. Note that generic letters or letters written by the applicant themselves have little value. - Many students write their own letters and have professors sign them. These have little values (we can easily recognize them) and can be considered as weaknesses. - Similarly, many professors write very generic letters about the students (a common example is that the students didn't do any research or make any impression for the professor to write about). These letters are also not useful and considered weak. - Many students get letters from someone from company where they did internship or are working at. It is OK as long as it is a research-based personalized letter. Though it is very rare to get such letters from companies (e.g., your supervisor is unlikely a researcher or have the background to assess your research ability).

PhD Admission committee takes LoRs very seriously and we read them carefully. For me personally, it's better to have a good personalized letter about your own research ability from someone who is less well-known than a generic/weak letter from a well-known person.

2.3. Your research (personal) statement.

While you might not be able to get good LoRs or change where your go to school, you have control of your statement! And you know we will read it, so write it well. Another reason for having a well-written LOR is that it allows us to evaluate how you communicate, which is very important in research. Moreover, it is important because if you need (GTA) funding, it will provide evidence that you can teach and communicate with students.

There are many guides on writing research statement (e.g., this one), so I will not talk too much about it. In short, convince us that you can do research through your previous/current experience, e.g., if you work on some projects, talk about it.

Finally, this is something that very easy to do, but is missed by many applicants: customize the statement for the school you're applying to, e.g., why do you apply there ? name some professors who you're interested in working with (this might help your letter get sent to them). Be careful not to send wrong statement to wrong school or mixing facts (e.g., talking about school X but mentioned wanting to work with profs. at school Y). I have seen such statements more time that I should.

For me I always read the research statement first and then LoRs. If I am persuaded by then, I would skim over other factors and advocate for acceptance (unless I see something so bad in other parts). If I am not convinced, then I will likely recommend rejection (unless I see something standout in other parts).

2.4. Your school

Attending top universities help a lot. However, most universities in Vietnam, even the top ones, are unknown to outsiders. Part of the reason is that Vietnamese universities aren't really active in the international research community, e.g., faculty do not publish in the top venues. So US faculty likely will not know much about the top schools in Vietnam or many other countries and just treat your school as "unknown foreign" (if your school is well-known, then it is "top foreign").

I've created a document containing the top schools in several countries and share it with other PhD admission committees.

2.5. Grades/GREs.

Having good grades in related courses is important, but of course not as important as other things such as LOR and statements above. However, in general, unless your school is well-known, having top grades/ranks usually will not help. This is simply because we cannot evaluate them. This is actually a big issue for students in top universities in Vietnam where the competition is so high that even students that ranked low are still very good. So what to do with this? well, apply to a place with a Vietnamse faculty! Otherwise you should put a note about this in your statement.

Not that while having good grades at unknown school does not help, having bad grades will generally hurt your case (unless your LoRs or statements give proper explaination). This is especially true if you have bad grades in CS/Math/Engineering courses.

Most CS programs in the US no longer require GREs, so you don't need to take them unless you think it will help prove something. You do need to take some standarized English test. Just do well enough to pass minimum requirement set by the university.

I typically just scan over your transcripts looking for "bad" grades in related courses. If you have very good grades from top schools that I know, I will also put a note on it.

5.2. Research Assistanship at School

  • The most common type is graduate assistanship, which is either graduate teaching assistant (GTA or TA) or graduate research assistant (GRA or RA).
  • Both come with tuition waiver, insurance, and stipend
  • Stipend is your salary
    • The amount of stipend depends on the university, which in turns depend on location (e.g., a stipend in Washington DC is likely higher than in Lincoln, Nebraska due to higher living cost)
    • Because a school year is (typically) 9-month in the US, so stipend is for 9 months (so divide by 9 for each month)
    • You have to pay tax on your stipend
    • Private universities might pay more for stipend
  • Some universities also pay insurance for spouse (or give very good discount)

5.2.1. GTA

  • is paid through the department
  • helps with teaching class (typically grading)
  • works with various courses and professors (not necessary their advisor)
  • Typically no summer because no school
  • Advice: GTA is common in the beginning, when you haven't found your advisor. But even if you get GRA, you should do GTA at least a semester or two, just to see how teaching is like.
Example: At GMU, when a student is admitted as a GRA, the benefits they received are tution waiver, insurance, a 9-month stipend of $28K + a summer stipend of $9K (for the 1st summer only).  GTA is guaranteed for 4 years.

5.2.2. GRA

  • is paid through a professor (typically your advisor)
  • works with a professor on a research project
  • Summer is possible, depending on the prof.
  • Advice: This is the most common type of funding! It is also ideal because you don't have to teach and so can focus on your research.

5.3. Fellowship

  • Another kind of funding is fellowship, in which the student applies from an external source (e.g., industries, government).
  • Fellowships are typically very competitive and generous, which covers pretty much everything that the research assistant ship at school covers, and gives even higher stipend (including summer).
    • Typically 3-5 years
    • Examples of such fellowships include the NSF Graduate Fellowship (needs to be a permanent resident or citizen) or Google Fellowship
  • Advice: Fellowship is very competitive, but that's why you will stand out if you get one. Not only you save your advisor lots of money yearly, it looks really good on your resume. Every Ph.D. in CS has pubs, but typically only superstars have NSF grad or Meta fellowship (in fact, these are so prestigious that even if you didn't get it but make it to the final round, you still should put it on your CV).
  • See this link for a large list of funding sources for Ph.D. students

5.4. Self-funded

  • Some Ph.D. students are funded through their company
    • The students work full time for the company and do their Ph.D. part-time
    • Typically will take longer (because you have to work full time) and require some serious committement from the students
  • Some Ph.D. students are funded through country, e.g., GMU has several students from Saudi Arabia that are funded through their government.

5.5. Others

  • There are also various kind of one-time thing and small awards from industry or organization, e.g., a $2000 award for the summer.
