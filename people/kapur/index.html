In Memoriam: Deepak Kapur

August 24, 1950 – April 11, 2026

Distinguished Professor, Department of Computer Science, University of New Mexico
Honorary Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, IIT Delhi

Deepak Kapur was a leading researcher in automated reasoning, term rewriting, and formal methods, and a longtime Distinguished Professor of Computer Science at the University of New Mexico (UNM). He passed away on April 11, 2026, in Albuquerque, New Mexico, at the age of 75, days after still sending research suggestions to a collaborator. This page collects his biography, career, research contributions, and tributes from colleagues, students, and friends.

Biography

Deepak Kapur was born on August 24, 1950, in Amritsar, Punjab, India. He attended school in Amritsar before earning admission to the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Kanpur, where he received a B.Tech. in Electrical Engineering (1971) and an M.Tech. in Computer Science (1973). He then went to the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), completing his Ph.D. in 1980 under the supervision of Barbara Liskov, with a dissertation titled “Towards a Theory of Abstract Data Types.”

After MIT, Kapur spent seven years as a Research Staff Member at General Electric Corporate Research and Development in Schenectady, NY (1980–1987), while also serving as an adjunct professor at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute. In 1988 he joined the University at Albany, SUNY as a tenured full professor, where he founded and directed the Institute for Programming and Logics and received the university's Excellence in Research Award (1998).

In 1998, Kapur moved to the University of New Mexico, where he chaired the Department of Computer Science from January 1999 to June 2006 — a period during which the department grew from a primarily teaching-focused unit into a research-active one, expanding TA support and recruiting a cohort of faculty who came to form the backbone of the department for decades. He remained a Distinguished Professor at UNM for the rest of his career, holding visiting and adjunct positions at the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research (Mumbai, from 2003), IIT Delhi (from 2012, later Honorary Professor), and the Institute of Software Science, Chinese Academy of Sciences (Beijing, from 2016). He spent his final sabbatical, in 2025, at NYU. He died on April 11, 2026, in Albuquerque, and is survived by his wife, Roli Varma, and their daughter, Ila.

Research & Contributions

Kapur's research spanned automated reasoning, term rewriting, unification theory, formal methods, program analysis, algebraic and geometric reasoning, elimination methods, resultants, constraint solving, distributed computing, and, with his wife Roli Varma, the social aspects of computing (particularly gender and ethnicity in CS/IT education). He was widely known for an encyclopedic knowledge of mathematics and for bridging computer algebra with automated deduction. Highlights include:

  • Rewrite Rule Laboratory (RRL): co-developed with Hantao Zhang, RRL was the first theorem prover built around term rewriting and the Knuth–Bendix completion procedure, and was widely used for hardware verification and specification analysis.
  • Groebner bases & geometric reasoning: foundational work on Comprehensive Groebner Bases and Dixon resultants, with applications to geometry theorem proving and computer vision.
  • Unification theory: results on AC-unification complexity and unification modulo theories of exponentiation, central to protocol and security analysis.
  • Induction and decision procedures: implicit induction methods and techniques for combining decision procedures, central to inductive theorem proving.
  • Loop invariant generation: work on automatically generating polynomial loop invariants using quantifier elimination and abstract interpretation, and co-development of DIG, a dynamic invariant generator for polynomial and array invariants (tool that Deepak and I kept developing together for over a decade).

Personal Life

Deepak Kapur is survived by his wife, Roli Varma, a longtime research collaborator with whom he co-authored several papers on gender and ethnicity in STEM education, including “Decoding Femininity in Computer Science in India” (CACM, 2015) and a study of brain drain and circulation among IIT graduates, and their daughter, Ila. He retained his Punjabi roots and accent throughout his decades in the US, and colleagues remember him as fiercely direct, endlessly curious, a lover of Scotch and a good story, and someone who continued to actively mentor students and pursue research — including learning about neural network verification, a field far from his original training — until his final days.

Tributes

The following remembrances were shared by colleagues, students, and friends after Deepak's passing.

It was a great shock when Roli told me about Deepak's passing. We were in the middle of an NSF proposal submission, and Deepak had just sent me suggestions days earlier. Despite his deteriorating health, he was still very active in research and eager to learn new things, and his thinking was very sharp in his last days as it was when he was in his prime.

In the beginning of my PhD study at UNM, I was working on program repair using genetic programming, and we were quite successful in fixing real bugs. However, Deepak was skeptical about the lack of formal specification. He kept asking about the meaning of “correct” and “bug,” which I could not provide, and this led him to question the work: “how can you fix what you don't understand?” Deepak transitioned me to his “rigorous” camp, and my PhD thesis is about dynamic invariant generation and more generally formal program analysis, which forms the basis of all my work since then.

Despite being a respected senior researcher, Deepak could be quite amusing and fun to be around. I always called him “Professor” (just Professor, not Professor Kapur) from the very beginning of my PhD. He found that amusing and started to call me “Professor” as well, which is how we addressed each other until I actually became a professor, at which point he started using my name. He also liked free food — once when a local paper ran an ad for a heavy lunch discount, I grabbed a stack of copies and we ate there for lunch for almost a month. Clearly mischievous, but also a lot of fun.

When my first paper with him received the Distinguished Paper award at ICSE — the top conference in software engineering — he said “that tells him a lot about the quality of SE research.” But that is how he was: brutally honest and never mincing his words, which of course could easily offend people. But if people could get past that, as I and many others who knew him well can attest, they would find that Deepak cared a lot for his colleagues, students, and especially about CS education and research — perhaps a bit too much, such that he got frustrated when he saw “junk” research or students lacking foundational knowledge.

Deepak is the type of person that I simply do not believe could “retire” and would think about research until his last day. I have been working on neural network verification (NNV) in recent years, and Deepak was quite interested in this line of research and tried to learn about it — at the age of 73, knowing he had just a couple of years left. Just a few weeks before he passed, I was describing to him a new paper on generating proofs to certify results of NNV tools, and he immediately observed that the resulting proofs can be used to modify the original networks and make them much smaller. I was excited about the idea and decided to invite him to be a co-PI on an NSF proposal I was submitting. His last message to me, sent just days before he passed, was about research: “but the checking only needs to be done on a certificate which is hopefully smaller.”

— ThanhVu (Vu) Nguyen, former PhD student; Professor, George Mason University

Deepak had a profound impact on UNM Computer Science. He joined the department in 1999 as Chair and helped transform it from primarily a teaching department into a research-active one. To support faculty research, he significantly increased the number of TA lines, providing essential teaching support and serving as an important recruiting tool for top graduate students. These changes also helped reduce faculty teaching loads, allowing more time for research.

I joined in 2004 and directly benefited from these efforts. As Chair, Deepak recruited Darko Stefanovic (former long-time department chair), Jared Saia, Patrick Bridges (CARC director), Melanie Moses, and myself, all of whom are now full professors and form the backbone of the department today.

Deepak truly loved both research and teaching. He cared deeply about his students and was dedicated to supporting their growth, both academically and personally. In a way, it may be a blessing that he passed while doing what he loved throughout his life.

Deepak's office and mine were just one door apart, and we were both usually early. For years I would walk down the empty hallway in Farris to my office, see his door ajar, and say, “Good morning, Deepak!” He would reply, “Good morning, Sir!” — even though he was far more senior than I am, he always responded that way. We would often chat over coffee, and sometimes a snack, in the mornings, sharing stories. He will be missed dearly by everyone in the department.

— Shuang (Sean) Luan, PhD, Professor and Chair, Department of Computer Science, University of New Mexico

I lost a dear friend and it is painful. He meant so much to me, both as a respected colleague and as a special human being. We first met when I came to UNM as Dean; later, we had offices close by and a deep friendship evolved. We shared similar values, we loved to analyze world affairs, we enjoyed retelling the story of our lives, and we encouraged each other to face challenges straight on. I loved our dinners together, especially those involving Roli's wonderful cooking, and I appreciated his collection of great Scotches, which he generously offered for side-by-side tasting. He enjoyed movies and often recommended hard-to-watch, traumatic stories about life in India.

Deepak loved both teaching and research. I think he was a perfectionist in both. He showed care for the individual while demanding quality work; he accepted no shortcuts on his part and invested much effort to ensure an excellent learning experience, even when facing major health challenges. On the research side, he felt a deep obligation toward his graduate students and their future careers. He was a great mentor and rejected retirement out of a desire to see his last student graduate. Professionally, he was an excellent researcher who continued to maintain research funding until the very end. His dedication to intellectual inquiry and innovation were central to his identity, and he could not envision a life without research.

I learned a lot from Deepak and will always think of him with affection. His passing is a great loss for our academic institution, his friends, and his family.

— Gruia-Catalin Roman, Former Dean, University of New Mexico

I am sad more than shocked. Every year when we met he seemed to have physically aged, but his conduct, both physical and mental, was so sharp that focus was never on the internal changes that were perhaps taking place. His involvements continued to be what they always had been — no letting off on any grounds. I met him first in the early 80s at the annual FST&TCS conferences. What stood out was that, unlike many others, he always submitted his better papers there. This was particularly impressive as there were not many among those who attended then with whom he could exchange ideas and/or collaborate. But he was always there. Looking back, I would say he helped lay the foundation of automated theorem proving in India.

Over the last fifteen or twenty years my memories of Deepak are more of a social nature — time spent together on the Delhi campus during his annual visits. I will miss his company, little discussions over sweet chai, and his aura. Rare persons like Deepak do not really go gently into the night. They leave too much behind, not only in our memories but in the collective living of the community, where his ideas will continue to challenge and inspire future generations for a long time.

— S. N. Maheshwari (Sachin), Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, IIT Delhi

Deepak was visiting me last summer and fall when he spent his sabbatical at NYU. Deepak told me not long ago that he and Roli were looking forward to spending more time in New York City. He and I had plans to continue our collaboration once he had time to fully settle in.

I first met Deepak at a Dagstuhl seminar many years ago. Since I was attending the seminar locally from Saarbrücken, I was there by car and gave him a ride back at the end of the week. The car was fully packed with PhD students, and Deepak did not mind at all. He was so kind and generous to us all. I always admired him — not only was he an exceptional scientist, but also such a truly wonderful and kind person. It is a great loss. We will dearly miss him.

— Thomas Wies, Professor, Department of Computer Science, NYU; collaborator during Deepak's sabbatical

Also, from IIT Delhi's in memoriam post, Prof. Sanjiva Prasad recalled:

Deepak was a regular visitor to the CSE Department, almost annually in the December–January period. He was ever enthusiastic about sharing his knowledge and insights. On almost every visit he would give a series of seminar talks, often running into hours or until his lungs got tired. A small question could lead to a comprehensive lecture on whatever mathematical domain it concerned, conveying insights both theoretical and computational. He was always accessible, and always ready to chat.

Deepak also had a fierce social commitment to progressive causes, and was unafraid to voice his opposition to autocratic politics across the world. … He had an easy, friendly attitude with everyone, and in all his years in the US, retained his “thhet” Punjabi accent.

— Sanjiva Prasad, Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, IIT Delhi
